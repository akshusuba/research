\begin{abstract}
Celiac disease (CeD) affects approximately 1\% of the global population and is associated with neurological manifestations including cerebellar ataxia, peripheral neuropathy, and cognitive dysfunction in 10--22\% of patients. However, the biological mechanisms connecting intestinal inflammation to brain pathology remain poorly understood. We present a computational approach using heterogeneous graph neural networks (HeteroGNNs) to model the celiac gut-brain axis. Our knowledge graph integrates 225 nodes across four biological entity types---gut microbes (n=14), microbial metabolites (n=12), immune/metabolic genes (n=113), and neurological phenotypes (n=86)---connected by 229 curated relationships. Using link prediction, our model achieves an AUROC of $0.905 \pm 0.050$ on gene-phenotype associations across five random seeds. Ablation studies demonstrate that removing metabolite nodes decreases performance by 4.8\%, while removing all intermediate nodes decreases performance by 6.0\%, indicating that multi-hop reasoning through metabolic intermediates contributes meaningfully to prediction accuracy.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} celiac disease, gut-brain axis, graph neural networks, knowledge graph, microbiome

\section{Introduction}

Celiac disease (CeD) is an autoimmune disorder triggered by dietary gluten in genetically susceptible individuals, primarily those carrying HLA-DQ2 or HLA-DQ8 haplotypes \citep{lebwohl2018celiac}. While traditionally characterized as an intestinal disease causing villous atrophy and malabsorption, CeD is increasingly recognized as a systemic condition with diverse extra-intestinal manifestations \citep{caio2019celiac}.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{gut_brain_axis.png}
    \caption{\textbf{The celiac gut-brain axis.} Gut microbiota produce neuroactive metabolites (including butyrate and other short-chain fatty acids) that travel through circulation and the vagus nerve to influence brain function. In celiac disease, intestinal inflammation and dysbiosis may disrupt this communication pathway, contributing to neurological manifestations.}
    \label{fig:gut_brain}
\end{figure}

Neurological complications represent a significant but underappreciated burden of celiac disease. Population-based studies report neurological symptoms in 10--22\% of celiac patients, including:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Gluten ataxia}: Cerebellar dysfunction with gait instability and dysarthria
    \item \textbf{Peripheral neuropathy}: Sensory and motor nerve damage
    \item \textbf{Cognitive impairment}: Memory difficulties and ``brain fog''
    \item \textbf{Psychiatric symptoms}: Depression and anxiety at 2--3$\times$ baseline rates
\end{itemize}

The mechanisms connecting intestinal inflammation to neurological symptoms remain incompletely understood. Three major hypotheses have been proposed:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Immune-mediated pathway}: Cross-reactive antibodies, particularly anti-transglutaminase 6 (TG6) antibodies, may target brain tissue \citep{hadjivassiliou2008autoantibodies}
    
    \item \textbf{Microbiome-mediated pathway}: Altered gut bacteria produce neuroactive metabolites (serotonin precursors, short-chain fatty acids) that influence brain function via the vagus nerve and systemic circulation \citep{dinan2017gutbrain}
    
    \item \textbf{Nutrient deficiency pathway}: Malabsorption of B vitamins, iron, and other micronutrients affects neurological function
\end{enumerate}

Understanding these complex, multi-step biological connections requires integrating diverse data types across multiple scales. Knowledge graphs provide a natural framework for representing heterogeneous biomedical relationships, and graph neural networks (GNNs) can learn from these complex structures to predict novel associations \citep{zhang2022graph}.

In this study, we construct a curated knowledge graph representing the celiac gut-brain axis and apply heterogeneous GNNs to predict gene-phenotype associations.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Knowledge Graph Construction}

We constructed a heterogeneous knowledge graph integrating four biological entity types relevant to celiac disease and gut-brain communication (Figure~\ref{fig:kg_schema}).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{kg_schema.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Knowledge graph schema.} The heterogeneous graph connects four node types: Microbes (gut bacteria altered in CeD), Metabolites (neuroactive compounds), Genes (immune and metabolic genes), and Phenotypes (neurological symptoms). Edges represent biological relationships including metabolite production, gene activation, and disease associations.}
    \label{fig:kg_schema}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Node Types}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Microbe nodes} (n=14): Gut bacteria with documented alterations in celiac disease, including \textit{Prevotella}, \textit{Bifidobacterium}, and \textit{Lactobacillus} species \citep{arcila2022microbiome}.
    
    \item \textbf{Metabolite nodes} (n=12): Neuroactive compounds produced by gut microbiota, including short-chain fatty acids (butyrate, propionate, acetate) and tryptophan metabolites (serotonin, kynurenine).
    
    \item \textbf{Gene nodes} (n=113): Human genes involved in immune function (HLA-DQ, interleukins, TNF), neurotransmitter metabolism (TPH1/2, SLC6A4, MAOA), and gut-brain signaling (FFAR2/3).
    
    \item \textbf{Phenotype nodes} (n=86): Human Phenotype Ontology (HPO) terms representing neurological symptoms associated with celiac disease.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Edge Types}

Relationships were curated from published literature and existing databases:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Microbe $\rightarrow$ Metabolite: 21 production/modulation edges
    \item Metabolite $\rightarrow$ Gene: 19 activation/transport edges
    \item Gene $\rightarrow$ Phenotype: 187 disease association edges (from Monarch Initiative)
\end{itemize}

The final knowledge graph contains 225 nodes and 227 edges. Figure~\ref{fig:kg_stats} shows the distribution of nodes and edges by type.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{kg_statistics.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Knowledge graph statistics.} \textbf{(a)} Node type distribution showing genes as the largest category (n=113), followed by phenotypes (n=86). \textbf{(b)} Edge type distribution showing gene-phenotype associations as the dominant edge type (n=187), with microbe-metabolite and metabolite-gene edges providing multi-hop connectivity.}
    \label{fig:kg_stats}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Heterogeneous Graph Neural Network}

We implemented a heterogeneous GNN using PyTorch Geometric \citep{fey2019pyg}. The architecture processes the multi-relational graph while respecting node and edge type heterogeneity (Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{gnn_architecture.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Heterogeneous GNN architecture for link prediction.} The input biomedical knowledge graph contains four node types (Microbes, Metabolites, Genes, Phenotypes) with associated embedding vectors. The graph is processed through two HeteroGNN layers that perform type-specific message passing. Final embeddings are used to predict links between gene and phenotype nodes.}
    \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Node Embeddings}

Each node is initialized with a learnable embedding vector $\mathbf{h}_i^{(0)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$, where $d=64$ is the embedding dimension. Separate embedding tables are maintained for each node type.

\subsubsection{Message Passing}

We employ two layers of heterogeneous graph convolutions based on GraphSAGE \citep{hamilton2017graphsage}. For each edge type $(s, r, t)$, the update rule is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{h}_j^{(\ell+1)} = \sigma\left(\mathbf{W}_r^{(\ell)} \cdot \text{AGGREGATE}\left(\{\mathbf{h}_i^{(\ell)} : i \in \mathcal{N}_r(j)\}\right)\right)
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{N}_r(j)$ denotes neighbors of node $j$ via relation $r$, $\mathbf{W}_r^{(\ell)}$ are relation-specific learnable weights, and $\sigma$ is the ReLU activation. Dropout ($p=0.3$) is applied for regularization.

\subsubsection{Link Prediction}

For gene-phenotype link prediction, we use a bilinear decoder:
\begin{equation}
    s_{ij} = \mathbf{h}_i^{(L)} \cdot \mathbf{W}_{decode} \cdot \mathbf{h}_j^{(L)}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{h}_i^{(L)}$ and $\mathbf{h}_j^{(L)}$ are final embeddings of gene $i$ and phenotype $j$.

\subsubsection{Training}

The model minimizes binary cross-entropy loss with 1:1 positive/negative edge sampling. We use the Adam optimizer (lr=0.01) for up to 100 epochs with early stopping (patience=20) on validation AUROC. All experiments use 5 random seeds.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Link Prediction Performance}

The heterogeneous GNN achieved strong performance on the gene-phenotype link prediction task. Training dynamics are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:training}.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{training_curves.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Training dynamics.} \textbf{(a)} Training loss decreases rapidly over epochs. \textbf{(b)} Validation AUROC and AUPRC converge to high values. The vertical dashed line indicates early stopping at epoch 5, with final test AUROC of 0.848 shown as horizontal dotted line.}
    \label{fig:training}
\end{figure}

Across five random seeds, the model achieved:

\caption{Link Prediction Performance (Mean $\pm$ Std over 5 Seeds)}
\label{tab:results}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Validation} & \textbf{Test} \\
\midrule
AUROC & $0.904 \pm 0.045$ & $0.905 \pm 0.050$ \\
AUPRC & $0.913 \pm 0.046$ & $0.896 \pm 0.038$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Training converged rapidly, with early stopping at a mean of $22.2 \pm 16.1$ epochs.

\subsection{Ablation Studies}

To quantify the contribution of different node types, we conducted systematic ablation experiments (Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}, Table~\ref{tab:ablation}).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{ablation_results.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Ablation study results.} Impact of removing node types on link prediction performance. The full graph (baseline) achieves AUROC of 0.905. Removing microbes decreases performance by 4.1\%, removing metabolites by 4.8\%, and using only direct gene-phenotype edges by 6.0\%. Error bars indicate standard deviation across 5 random seeds.}
    \label{fig:ablation}
\end{figure}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Ablation Study Results (Mean $\pm$ Std over 5 Seeds)}
\label{tab:ablation}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Configuration} & \textbf{AUROC} & \textbf{AUPRC} & \textbf{$\Delta$AUROC} \\
\midrule
Full graph (baseline) & $0.905 \pm 0.050$ & $0.896 \pm 0.038$ & -- \\
Remove microbes & $0.868 \pm 0.096$ & $0.852 \pm 0.082$ & $-4.1\%$ \\
Remove metabolites & $0.861 \pm 0.034$ & $0.858 \pm 0.043$ & $-4.8\%$ \\
Direct gene-phenotype only & $0.850 \pm 0.061$ & $0.841 \pm 0.096$ & $-6.0\%$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Key findings:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Metabolites contribute meaningfully}: Removing metabolite nodes decreased AUROC by 4.8\%, indicating that neuroactive compounds provide useful signal for predictions.
    
    \item \textbf{Multi-hop reasoning helps}: The full model outperforms the direct gene-phenotype model by 6.0\%, suggesting the GNN learns to propagate information through biological intermediates.
    
    \item \textbf{Microbe contribution is indirect}: Removing microbe nodes alone had a smaller effect (4.1\%), possibly because their information is captured through metabolite products.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion}

Our results demonstrate that heterogeneous graph neural networks can effectively model the complex relationships in the celiac gut-brain axis. The high prediction accuracy (AUROC = 0.905) suggests the model captures meaningful biological signal.

\subsection{Biological Interpretation}

The ablation studies provide quantitative evidence for the importance of metabolic intermediates in gut-brain communication. The 4.8\% performance drop when removing metabolite nodes supports the hypothesis that neuroactive metabolites---including serotonin (95\% produced in the gut), short-chain fatty acids, and tryptophan derivatives---play a role in connecting intestinal changes to neurological symptoms.

This aligns with clinical observations that celiac patients show altered tryptophan metabolism \citep{russo2012tryptophan} and that gluten-free diet often improves both gastrointestinal and neurological symptoms.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Small graph size}: With 225 nodes and 227 edges, our knowledge graph is small compared to comprehensive biomedical KGs. This limits pattern complexity.
    
    \item \textbf{Curation bias}: Manual curation may favor well-studied relationships.
    
    \item \textbf{Correlation vs.\ causation}: Link prediction identifies statistical associations, not causal mechanisms.
    
    \item \textbf{Variance}: The standard deviation of $\pm 0.050$ indicates sensitivity to random initialization.
\end{enumerate}

% ============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}

We developed a graph-based machine learning approach to model the celiac gut-brain axis using heterogeneous graph neural networks. Our curated knowledge graph integrates microbiome, metabolite, gene, and phenotype data, achieving AUROC of $0.905 \pm 0.050$ on gene-phenotype link prediction.

Ablation studies demonstrate that metabolic intermediates contribute meaningfully to prediction accuracy, providing computational support for the role of neuroactive metabolites in gut-brain communication.
